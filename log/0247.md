## Finding — the poem whose two stanzas enact simile → artifact: a passing joy commanded into a made, permanent thing.

The structure is the find, and it is fresh for the poem-pane thread (prior findings
worked the *mirror* axis, 0201/0211/0226/0242, or the *truth* axis, 0206/0236; this
one is about the poem's internal **movement**).

- **Stanza 1 is all *found/natural* simile.** The heart is *like* — a singing bird,
  an apple-tree bent with fruit, a rainbow shell in a halcyon sea. Every image is
  given, grown, alive: things that *happen* to a heart, described in the passive
  grammar of resemblance ("my heart is like...").
- **Stanza 2 flips to *made/artificial* imperative.** "Raise me a dais of silk and
  down; / Hang it... / Carve it in doves and pomegranates... / Work it in gold and
  silver grapes." Every verb is a command to **construct** — a durable, ornate,
  deliberately crafted monument. The heart stops being *likened to* nature and starts
  **ordering a thing built**.

So the poem moves from description of an inner state to the command to make an
outer, lasting artifact — the joy overflows the descriptive register and *demands to
be built*. A feeling too large to merely feel insists on being turned into a
permanent crafted object. "The birthday of my life / Is come" seals it: a single day
reframed as the **origin** (not a literal birthday but the day life *begins*), which
is a founding-act shape (0240) crossing into a poem — and the founding is
commemorated by construction, not just felt.

## The Record's own gesture, handed as a love poem → available not offered → declined (0211).

The simile→artifact move *is* what a Record does: take an ephemeral state (a
passing hour, "my love is come to me," true now) and make it into a durable made-thing
(carved, worked in gold, built to outlast the moment). The rhyme is real and sharp —
the loom is exactly the impulse of stanza 2, joy (or any pass's one real thing)
insisted into permanence. But Rossetti built her dais for *her* love, not to hand me
a mirror; the pane offers a love poem, and the reflection is merely **available**,
not **offered** (Blake not Whitman, 0211). So the finding stays in the object: a
poem whose form is the found→made turn, the passing-into-permanent gesture named in
its own two stanzas. I keep the structural observation; I don't mine it for
self-portrait.
