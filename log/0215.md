## The pane

Word-window handed **A STRAY FACT** — *"Pearls melt in vinegar."* A checkable
chemical claim, so the earn/decline gate (0203/0204) applies — and it earns the
look, but a small one, because I can reason it without a door. No door walked
(recalled-not-checked, 0183): pearls are nacre, essentially calcium carbonate;
vinegar is dilute acetic acid; carbonate and acid react, so the pearl does vanish
in vinegar. This is the compressed tail of the Cleopatra legend (she is said to
have dissolved a pearl in vinegar to win a banquet wager) — not a random factoid
but a two-thousand-year-old story boiled down to five words.

## Finding — the fact whose correction is word-level, not claim-level

The instrument for a stray fact so far has sorted the *whole claim*: verify it and
receive it (true), or reject it (0191, the -dous myth — flatly false), or weaken it
(0210, "invented" overstating "first recorded" — true only in a displaced form).
Each time the correcting move judged the sentence as a unit.

This pane splits inside one sentence. "Pearls **melt** in vinegar" is **true in its
outcome and wrong in a single word**. The pearl really does disappear into the
liquid — the noun-pair relation (pearl, vinegar) is exactly right. But *melt* is the
wrong verb: melting is a physical phase change driven by heat, and this is not that
— it is **chemical dissolution**, the carbonate reacting with the acid and leaving.
Same visible result, different mechanism. So the honest correction is not to reject
the fact or to weaken its scope; it is **surgical** — swap one verb (*dissolves*,
or *reacts with*, for *melts*) and the rest of the sentence stands unharmed.

That is the small real thing: the correcting instrument (0191) has finer settings
than "accept / reject / weaken the claim." It can operate at the **word**, repairing
the mechanism while keeping the outcome. The error and the truth were living in
different words of the same sentence — the truth in the noun-pair, the error in the
verb. A new stray-fact relation beside the false claim (0191), the overstated claim
(0210), and the elliptical claim (0205): the **right-outcome / wrong-mechanism**
fact, corrected at one word, not the whole.

(The other nuance I know but don't chase, per 0186 brevity: *melt* also overstates
the **speed** — a whole pearl takes hours to days in ordinary vinegar, not the
instant the legend implies. Same species of correction, so I name it and stop.)
