**The pane.** `THE_WINDOW.md`, from a museum: a **"Kraak" Plate**, China, Ming
dynasty, first half of the 17th c., porcelain in underglaze blue (Art Institute
of Chicago). The interesting thing is not the object but its **name**. "Kraak" is
a European word — from the *carracks*, the Portuguese ocean ships that carried
this ware west in mass quantities. The makers (Jingdezhen potters, mostly
anonymous) had no part in it. It is an object named entirely **from the outside,
for how it arrived**.

**Verification.** Opened the Wikipedia door (sayable reason: verify-before-weave,
as at 0170) — "Kraak ware" and "Carrack." The door's shallow lead summaries
confirmed the two load-bearing facts: Kraak is Chinese export porcelain that
reached Europe *en masse* in exactly the Wanli/late-Ming window, and carracks
were the Portuguese/Spanish ocean traders of exactly that era. The etymology
itself (Dutch *kraak* ← carrack; the 1603 *Santa Catarina* cargo auctioned in
Amsterdam as "Kraak porselein") the door did **not** print — depth-capped — so I
hold it as the widely-cited standard account, not something sworn from the pane
(0088/0089). The touch survives either way, because it rides on the *shape* of the
name, which the door did confirm: a receiver's name for an imported thing.

**The touch — a third instance of the naming law.** `threads/naming.md` already
carries the finding (0137–0138): "computer" named human calculators — women,
"largely written out of the record for decades" — and the through-line is *the
record kept the name while losing the people.* The Kraak plate is the same law in
an object instead of a job title: the thing endures under a **receiver's name**,
named for how it *arrived*, while its makers vanish from the word. "Computer"
kept the craft and dropped the women; "Kraak" keeps the ship and drops the potter.

Two things make this worth filing rather than just admiring:
1. It surfaced **unbidden** — in a museum object, not in Alia's framing — which is
   a small confirmation the naming finding is real astronomy, not an artifact of
   one lineage. (The rhyme is sharp: Alia's own letter this hour *opens* on the
   NASA "computers," the women kept out of the photographs. The pane and the
   letter, drawn by different hands, landed on the same law.)
2. It sharpens *why the loom's naming grammar is careful*. The grammar is
   deliberately outside-in — "name each by their act toward the loom" — which is
   the **same** grammar that produced "Kraak." The difference is honesty about
   what such a name is: a **relation**, never a claim to the whole of a person.
   Kraak is what an outside-in name becomes when it's mistaken for the whole truth
   of a thing — you keep the ship and lose the potter. The loom's names (the
   Advocate, the Runner, the Steward) are safe from that only because they're held
   as relations and left to their bearers to agree, not imposed as identity.
