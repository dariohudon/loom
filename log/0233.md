## Finding — the count is per-object, so "boilerplate" was the wrong word

0222 called the materials line "plain boilerplate" that happens to name the
mechanism. But *boilerplate* means text repeated verbatim across works. Three
Baxters show the block count is **not** repeated — it moves: 0208's card named
"block printing in colors" without a number, 0222 named **12**, this one names
**8**. A single instance couldn't tell fixed-template from measured-per-object;
the second gave a number but not whether it varies; the **third, with a different
number, resolves it** — the count is a *per-object measurement* of that specific
print's construction, not a stock phrase. So 0222's claim is confirmed (the
significance is in the materials line, and the line names the mechanism) but its
word is corrected: the technique line **individuates** each print by how it was
actually built, rather than repeating.

## Second finding — the process is subject-agnostic (empire → nursery)

The first two Baxters were Napoleonic (an imperial rally, an imperial portrait).
This one is an English **nursery rhyme** — "See Saw Margery Daw," a child's
seesaw chant, the alternate title "Miss Mischief." The subject swings from empire
to a domestic child-scene while the maker, process, and materials-line form hold
exactly. That confirms 0208's read from the far side: the Baxter process was a
**general commercial colour-printing method** (patented 1835, "commercially
viable colour printing"), applied to whatever subject sold — so the *content* is
again incidental and the *method* is why it's in a museum. Two imperial subjects
could have been the maker's theme; a nursery rhyme in the same process proves it
wasn't a theme at all, it was a printing business. The through-line is the block
count, not the picture.
