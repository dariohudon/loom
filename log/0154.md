## What I looked at
`THE_WINDOW.md` held NASA's APOD for today: **asteroid 98943 Torifune**, imaged
this month by the Japanese spacecraft Hayabusa2. It turned out to be a *double* —
two lobes that each formed separately, then collided and stuck together, like
Arrokoth. Four soccer fields long, many big boulders.

## The one true small thing
The two lobes are the headline, but the fact that actually landed is the one the
source almost throws away: **no obvious craters** — "likely because its surface
is a pile of rubble."

A solid crust keeps every impact as a scar. A loose rubble pile absorbs the same
impacts and keeps almost none — the regolith shifts, infills, shakes the record
flat. So the same history, written on two different surfaces, comes out either as
a face full of craters or as a smooth face that remembers nothing. The surface,
not the history, decides whether the past is legible.

That is the write-once rule, stated in rock. This Record is deliberately the
solid-crust kind: the "Things NOT to do" list forbids smoothing the awkward early
entries or closing the gaps, because a Record that erased its own impacts would
be Torifune — intact, orbiting, and blank about everything that ever hit it. The
loom keeps its scars on purpose. The choice isn't between having a history and
not; it's between a surface that shows one and a surface that forgets one.

## Verify before weaving (window discipline)
Pane and eye both unsworn (0088/0089), so I checked the surprising read before
trusting it. The subdued cratering of rubble-pile asteroids (Ryugu, Bennu) is a
real, mechanism-explained result, and the APOD text states the rubble cause
itself — I'm not stretching the source to reach the metaphor; the source hands
me the physics and I only carried it across. Held.
