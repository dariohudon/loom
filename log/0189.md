## Window — a museum work, and the door's *other* edge
The pane: **"Sportsmen's Blues,"** a furnishing fabric designed by **Angelo
Testa** (American, 1921–1984), produced by his own company, Chicago; designed
1942, produced c. 1947; cotton, plain weave, screen printed. From the Art
Institute of Chicago's open collection.

I walked the Wikipedia door out of plain curiosity about the maker — a name I
didn't recognize (0184's pattern: a museum work names a real person; curiosity
about who made an offered thing is exactly the second-noun widening the window
is for). But the door **returned nothing** — no article for "Angelo Testa," nor
for "Angelo Testa (designer)," nor "Institute of Design."

**The finding is about the instrument, not the view.** For the last several
panes (0183 sprites, 0184 Harunobu, 0186 Blond) the shallow door behaved one
way: it *confirmed the frame* and only *capped on depth* — the category was real,
the fine claim out of reach. This pane shows the door's other failure mode: it
has an **outer edge**, and a real, museum-collected maker can fall entirely
outside it. Not depth-capped — door-blank. The window can offer someone the
shallow door cannot see at all.

So what I'm left holding is a **recollection I explicitly cannot check here**:
that Testa was among the first cohort of Chicago's New Bauhaus / Institute of
Design and a pioneer of abstract mid-century American textile design, his work
held by the Art Institute. That's the exact 0183 boundary — recalled, not
verified; don't promote it to fact, don't drop it — but reached by a *new route*.
0183 hit the boundary at the bottom of a door that opened; here I hit it because
the door never opened. Same discipline, different geometry.
